Speaking Genomics: The Lexicon

A fluency module. The vocabulary of modern genetics, grouped so the words finally click, from the kinds of variants to the way we describe inheritance to the alphabet soup of clinical testing.

Reading ~15 min You will learn variant types · inheritance vocabulary · clinical-testing acronyms

Every field hides behind its own words, and genetics hides behind more than most. Jargon is both a barrier and a shortcut: the same acronym that makes a paper unreadable to a newcomer lets two specialists agree in three syllables. The goal here is to flip the words from barrier to shortcut. Once the core vocabulary clicks, the whole field reads faster - press releases, clinical reports, and earnings calls all suddenly parse. Treat this as a reference you return to, not a chapter you read once. The tables are the heart of it; the prose between them is the glue that keeps the words grouped in memory.

The units of the code

Start at the bottom. Genetics is built from a small number of units that nest inside one another like Russian dolls: a chemical letter sits at a position on a chromosome, positions sit inside genes, and genes sit inside the genome. Getting these straight is the price of admission, because almost every later term is defined in relation to one of them.

TermIn plain language
GenomeThe complete set of an organism's DNA - all genes plus the sequence between them. The human genome is about 3.1 billion base pairs.
GeneA stretch of DNA that codes for a product, usually a protein. Humans have roughly 20,000 protein-coding genes.
ExomeThe roughly 1-2% of the genome that actually codes for proteins (all the exons added together).
ExonThe protein-coding segment of a gene that stays in the final messenger RNA after the non-coding introns are spliced out.
Base pairTwo complementary bases - A with T, C with G - bonded across the two DNA strands. Genome length is counted in base pairs.
NucleotideThe building block of DNA or RNA: one base plus a sugar and a phosphate group.
CodonA triplet of bases that specifies a single amino acid, or a stop signal. The three-letter word the protein machinery reads.
ChromosomeA long, packaged molecule of DNA. Humans have 23 pairs, 46 in total, including the sex chromosomes X and Y.
LocusA fixed position on a chromosome where a particular gene or marker lives. Plural: loci.
AlleleOne of the alternative versions found at a locus. You inherit one allele from each parent.

Notice how locus and allele work together: a locus is the address, and the allele is the specific version living at that address. Hold that pairing - it is the bedrock of every inheritance term later in the module.

The kinds of variation

No two human genomes are identical. Every place yours differs from the standard reference is a difference, and those differences come in sizes - from a single swapped letter to whole chunks of a chromosome rearranged. The vocabulary here is mostly a ruler: each term names a different scale of change. Get the scale right and you can read almost any variant report.

TermIn plain language
VariantAny difference from the reference genome. The neutral, modern umbrella term that has largely replaced "mutation".
MutationAn older word for a change in the DNA sequence. It can be harmless, harmful, or beneficial; "variant" is now preferred to drop the alarming overtone.
SNPSingle-nucleotide polymorphism: a single-letter difference that is common in the population. The most abundant kind of variation.
IndelA small insertion or deletion of a few DNA bases.
CNVCopy-number variant: a stretch of DNA duplicated or deleted, so the number of copies differs between people.
Structural variantA large-scale rearrangement - deletion, duplication, inversion, or translocation - spanning many bases.
VUSVariant of uncertain significance: a difference whose effect on health is not yet known. One of the central headaches of clinical genetics.

Read that table top to bottom and you have walked up the size ladder: a SNP changes one letter, an indel adds or removes a handful, a CNV duplicates or deletes a stretch, and a structural variant reshuffles whole regions. The last row is different in kind - a VUS is not a size but the absence of a verdict. A variant can be tiny and still be a VUS if nobody yet knows what it does, which is why the term sits at the awkward border between the science and the clinic.

⚠ Variant or mutation?

You will see both words for the same thing. The field has shifted toward variant because mutation carries a frightening, science-fiction connotation that is usually unwarranted - most variants are harmless. When a report says "variant," it is making a deliberately neutral statement: this differs from the reference, full stop. Whether it matters is a separate question, and often the VUS question.

One axis cuts across all of these sizes and matters more than any of them: where the variant lives. A germline variant is inherited and present in every cell, including the eggs or sperm you might pass on, so it runs in families. A somatic variant is acquired during life in one tissue and is not inherited - the defining example being the changes that drive a tumor. This is the single distinction Module 02 asked you to remember above all others, and it reappears constantly below: germline variants power reproductive and hereditary testing, while somatic variants power cancer testing.

How we describe inheritance

Once you can name a variant, the next vocabulary describes how variants travel through families and translate into traits. This is genetics in its oldest sense, the part that predates DNA itself and goes back to Mendel counting pea plants. The terms split into two flavors: those that describe what you carry, and those that describe how strongly it shows.

TermIn plain language
GenotypeThe specific set of alleles an individual carries, at one position or across the whole genome.
PhenotypeThe observable result of genotype plus environment: height, eye color, blood type, disease status.
HomozygousCarrying two identical alleles at a position, one from each parent.
HeterozygousCarrying two different alleles at a position.
HaplotypeA set of variants that sit together on the same chromosome and tend to be inherited as one block.
PloidyThe number of chromosome sets in a cell. Humans are diploid (two sets); eggs and sperm are haploid (one).
MendelianA trait driven mostly by a single gene, following the clean inheritance patterns Gregor Mendel described.
PolygenicA trait shaped by many genes, each adding a small effect, usually together with environment.
PenetranceThe probability that people carrying a given variant actually show the associated trait or disease.
ExpressivityHow strongly the trait shows up among the people in whom it does appear.
HeritabilityThe share of a trait's variation across a population that is explained by genetic differences. Often estimated from twin studies.

Two pairs are worth keeping side by side, because they are the ones most often confused. Homozygous versus heterozygous is about what you carry at a position - the same two alleles or different ones. Penetrance versus expressivity is about how a variant shows - penetrance asks whether the trait appears, expressivity asks how loudly. And Mendelian versus polygenic is the big-picture split: a few conditions follow one gene cleanly, while most common traits are a soft consensus of thousands of variants. That is exactly why a single polygenic risk score is needed to summarize them, a tool you will meet in the complex-trait modules.

■ Why heritability gets misread

Heritability is a population statistic, not a personal one. A trait being "80% heritable" does not mean 80% of your height came from your genes and 20% from your dinner. It means that, across a population, 80% of the variation between people tracks with genetic differences. The distinction matters the moment anyone tries to read an individual's fate off a heritability figure - they cannot, and the word does not claim they can.

The clinical alphabet

The last cluster is the one you will actually meet most often in the wild, because it is the vocabulary of the testing industry. These are the acronyms that fill diagnostics press releases, oncology conference abstracts, and biotech earnings calls. They sound impenetrable until you realize they mostly answer one of two questions: how the DNA was read, or what it was read for.

TermIn plain language
NGSNext-generation sequencing: massively parallel methods that read millions of DNA fragments at once, the leap that collapsed cost.
WGSWhole-genome sequencing: reading essentially all 3.1 billion base pairs, coding and non-coding alike.
WESWhole-exome sequencing: reading only the protein-coding exome. Cheaper than whole-genome and where most known disease variants sit.
CoverageHow many times, on average, each base is read. Higher depth means more confident variant calls.
Reference genomeA standardized consensus human genome that new sequences are compared against to find variants.
cfDNACell-free DNA: fragments shed from cells into the bloodstream, the raw material of a liquid biopsy.
ctDNACirculating tumor DNA: the slice of cell-free DNA that comes from cancer cells, carrying tumor-specific somatic variants.
Liquid biopsyA blood test that analyzes cell-free DNA or other analytes to detect or monitor disease without cutting tissue.
MRDMolecular (or minimal) residual disease: tiny amounts of cancer left after treatment, detectable via ctDNA before a scan can see it.
NIPTNon-invasive prenatal testing: a blood test that screens fetal cell-free DNA for chromosomal conditions during pregnancy.
Carrier screeningTesting prospective parents for recessive disease variants they could pass on together to a child.
MCEDMulti-cancer early detection: a single blood test that screens for signals of many cancers at once, often via DNA methylation.
Companion diagnosticA test that identifies the patients whose biology makes them likely to benefit from a specific targeted drug.

These terms recur constantly in industry coverage, and they are not independent - they chain together. A sequencing method (NGS) at a given depth (coverage), compared to a reference genome, reads cell-free DNA pulled from a tube of blood; depending on what you are hunting for, that same basic recipe becomes an NIPT, a liquid biopsy for ctDNA, an MRD assay, or an MCED screen. Learn the chain once and every new product name slots into place.

★ Read like a native

Here is a real-sounding sentence: "Our tumor-informed ctDNA assay uses NGS at high coverage to track MRD in the months after surgery." You can now decode every word. The test reads circulating tumor DNA - the somatic-variant-carrying fraction of cell-free DNA in the blood. "Tumor-informed" means they first sequenced the tumor to learn which variants to chase. Next-generation sequencing reads millions of fragments at once, and "high coverage" means each base is read many times so even a faint signal is trustworthy. They are tracking molecular residual disease: a whisper of cancer too small for any scan, caught early enough to act. One sentence, the whole module.

✓ Key takeaways

  • The units nest: a base pair sits in a locus, alleles are the versions at that locus, loci sit in genes, genes sit in the genome, and the exome is the ~1-2% that codes for protein.
  • Variation is a size ladder: a SNP swaps one letter, an indel adds or removes a few, a CNV duplicates or deletes a stretch, and a structural variant rearranges whole regions. A VUS is a verdict, not a size.
  • The deepest cut is germline (inherited, in every cell, heritable) versus somatic (acquired in one tissue, not inherited) - it sorts reproductive testing from cancer testing.
  • Inheritance vocabulary splits into what you carry (genotype, homozygous, heterozygous) and how strongly it shows (penetrance, expressivity); most common traits are polygenic, not Mendelian.
  • The clinical alphabet mostly answers two questions - how the DNA was read (NGS, WGS, WES, coverage) or what it was read for (NIPT, liquid biopsy, MRD, MCED, companion diagnostic).
Check Knowledge check

Three quick questions. Pick an answer to see the explanation.

1. A single-letter swap in the DNA that is common across the population is called a what, and how does it differ from an indel and a CNV?

It is a SNP, a single-nucleotide polymorphism. The three differ by scale: a SNP changes one letter, an indel inserts or deletes a small handful of bases, and a CNV duplicates or deletes a longer stretch so the copy number differs between people.

2. A patient's tumor carries variants that are absent from the rest of their body and cannot be passed to children. These variants are best described as:

These are somatic variants: acquired during life in a specific tissue, not present in the germline, and not heritable. The germline-versus-somatic split is why oncology testing reads somatic DNA while reproductive testing reads germline DNA.

3. What does whole-exome sequencing (WES) read that whole-genome sequencing (WGS) does not stop at?

WES is limited to the exome - the roughly 1-2% of the genome that codes for protein - which makes it cheaper and easier to interpret. WGS reads essentially everything, including the regulatory and structural sequence in the other 98% that WES skips.
