Part I - Foundations - Module 01

Orientation: The Big Map

A ten-minute aerial view of the whole field, so the rest of the course has a frame to hang on. What genetics asks, how genomics differs, the arc from Mendel to AI, and how to navigate these fifteen modules.

Reading ~10 min You will learn the landscape · the key questions · how to use the course

This course is a guided tour, for a smart non-biologist, from the chemistry of DNA all the way to the artificial-intelligence systems now reshaping the field. It is built on a simple belief: you do not need a lab coat to understand genetics, you need a good map. Over fifteen short modules we blend the science with the history that produced it and the industry that now runs on it, because the three only make full sense together. This first module is the map itself - the part you keep in your head while every later module fills in a region.

Genetics versus genomics

People use the two words loosely, but the distinction is real and it organizes everything that follows. Genetics is the older discipline: the study of single genes and how traits pass from parent to child. It begins with a monk and his garden. In 1865 Gregor Mendel, breeding pea plants and counting their offspring, showed that inheritance follows tidy mathematical rules - one gene, one trait, passed in predictable ratios. For nearly a century that one-gene-at-a-time view was all anyone could manage, and it remains the right lens for Mendelian conditions like cystic fibrosis or sickle-cell disease.

Genomics is the modern, wholesale version. Instead of studying one gene, it studies the entire genome at once - all of an organism's DNA, read and compared in bulk. This was simply impossible until the cost of sequencing collapsed around the year 2000. Genomics is what lets researchers ask why most common traits are polygenic, shaped by thousands of small variants rather than one. Genetics gave us the grammar; genomics gave us the whole library and the means to scan it.

Genetics

Single genes and heredity. Born with Mendel's peas (1865). One gene, one trait, predictable ratios. The right lens for inherited, single-gene disorders.

Genomics

The whole genome at once, studied in bulk. Made possible by cheap sequencing (~2000). The lens for variation, complex traits, and population-scale questions.

The five questions the field keeps asking

Strip away the jargon and almost every project in genetics is chasing one of five questions. Keep these in mind and you will always know which region of the map you are standing in.

  1. What is in the code? Reading the sequence of letters in a genome. This is sequencing, from Sanger's first method to today's next-generation sequencing.
  2. What does it do? Working out the function of a gene - what protein it makes and what that protein does in a living cell.
  3. How do we differ? Cataloguing variation: the variants that make one person's genome differ from the next, studied at scale through methods like the genome-wide association study.
  4. What goes wrong? Linking variation to disease - which changes in the code cause illness, and through what mechanism, whether inherited (germline) or acquired (somatic).
  5. Can we rewrite it? Editing the code deliberately. The arrival of CRISPR in 2012 turned this from science fiction into a routine laboratory tool and, increasingly, a medicine.
■ Why this framing pays off

Each of the five questions has become an industry. Sequencing built the instrument makers; variation and disease built clinical testing; rewriting built gene-therapy biotech. When you read about a genomics company later in the course, ask which of the five it sells, and its strategy snaps into focus.

A 160-year arc on one page

The field moved from counting pea plants to predicting protein shapes with neural networks in about a century and a half. Here is the whole journey compressed into the milestones that actually changed what was possible.

YearMilestoneWhy it mattered
1865Mendel's laws of inheritanceShowed heredity follows countable rules, founding genetics decades before anyone knew DNA existed.
1953Watson, Crick and Franklin reveal DNA's double helixThe structure explained how DNA copies itself and carries information - the molecular basis of heredity.
1960sThe genetic code is crackedResearchers learned how three-letter words of DNA specify amino acids, linking sequence to protein.
1977Sanger sequencing inventedThe first practical way to read DNA letter by letter, the workhorse method for the next 30 years.
1990-2003The Human Genome ProjectA 13-year, multinational effort delivered the first reference human genome, the field's foundational map.
~2007-2014Next-generation sequencing and the $1,000 genomeMassively parallel reading crashed the cost of a genome from billions to about a thousand dollars, making genomics routine.
2012CRISPR gene editingA cheap, programmable way to cut and edit DNA turned genome editing into an everyday tool.
2020AlphaFold predicts protein structureAn AI system solved the decades-old problem of predicting a protein's 3D shape from its sequence.
2024Nobel Prize for AI protein workThe Chemistry prize recognized computational protein structure and design, marking AI's arrival in the life sciences.
⚠ Read the slope, not just the dates

Notice how the gaps shrink. It took 88 years to get from Mendel to the double helix, but only a few years to go from cheap sequencing to programmable editing to AI-predicted structures. The pace of the field is accelerating, which is exactly why the last modules of this course look forward, not back.

How to use this course

Genetics 101 is organized into 6 parts and 15 modules, about 3 hours of reading in total, fully self-paced. You can finish it in an afternoon or spread it across a fortnight. Each module is short, illustrated, and designed to stand close to the others while still making sense on its own.

A few interactive features are worth knowing about before you dive in. Any vocabulary word with a dotted underline is a glossary term - hover over it on a computer, or tap it on a phone, and a short definition appears. Every module ends with a short knowledge check so you can confirm an idea landed before moving on. Where a module includes a chart, it is interactive: hover over a bar or point to read the exact value behind it.

★ How to navigate

Read the modules in order if you can - each one assumes the vocabulary of the ones before it, and the early foundations make everything later far easier. That said, three modules are written to stand alone if a topic pulls you in: Modules 13 and 14 on clinical diagnostics and the Natera case study, and Module 15 on artificial intelligence in genetics. If you only have twenty minutes and a specific interest, start there. Otherwise, begin at the beginning.

One last orienting thought. You will meet a great deal of machinery in this course - the molecule of DNA, the message of RNA, the distinction between genotype and phenotype, the statistics of heritability, and the neural networks behind AlphaFold. None of it is harder than what you already hold in your head about, say, how a city works. It is just unfamiliar. The map you have just read is enough to keep you from getting lost; the rest of the course simply walks the territory.

Check Knowledge check

Three quick questions. Pick an answer to see the explanation.

1. What best captures the difference between genetics and genomics?

Genetics is the older study of single genes and how traits are inherited, dating to Mendel in 1865. Genomics looks at the whole genome in bulk and only became practical once sequencing grew cheap around 2000.

2. Which of these milestones came first chronologically?

Mendel published his laws of inheritance in 1865, long before the others: the Human Genome Project ran 1990-2003, CRISPR editing arrived in 2012, and AlphaFold's structure predictions came in 2020.

3. What single development made genomics - studying the whole genome at scale - possible?

Genomics needs to read entire genomes affordably. The collapse in sequencing cost - from billions of dollars to roughly a thousand for a genome - is what turned genome-scale study from a one-off feat into a routine tool.
