Genomics in the Clinic

This is where the science becomes an industry. Liquid biopsy, circulating tumor DNA, residual-disease monitoring, and multi-cancer early detection are turning a tube of blood into a window on disease, and into one of the fastest-growing markets in medicine.

Reading ~14 min You will learn liquid biopsy · ctDNA · MRD · MCED · the competitive map

Three threads from earlier modules converge here into real products and a real market. The cost of sequencing collapsed, so reading DNA at scale became cheap. The distinction between inherited germline DNA and acquired somatic DNA told us what we were reading and why it mattered. And the biology of cell-free DNA - fragments of genome floating in the blood - gave us something to read without ever cutting tissue. Put those together and you get a blood test that can find, profile, and follow cancer. That is the business of modern molecular diagnostics, and it is the subject of this module.

The tube of blood

Your cells are not sealed boxes. As they live, divide, and die, they constantly shed short fragments of DNA into the bloodstream. Collectively this is cell-free DNA, or cfDNA - billions of fragments circulating at any moment, mostly from ordinary turnover of healthy tissue. A tumor does the same thing. The portion of cfDNA that comes from cancer cells, carrying the tumor's somatic variants, is called circulating tumor DNA, or ctDNA.

A liquid biopsy is simply the analysis of this material from a routine blood draw. Instead of a surgeon cutting out a piece of tumor for a traditional tissue biopsy - invasive, slow, and sometimes impossible to reach - a phlebotomist fills a tube, and a lab sequences the DNA inside it. The appeal is obvious: it is safe, it can be repeated as often as you like, and it samples DNA shed from across the whole body rather than one needle's worth of one site.

The catch is in that last sentence. In a patient with cancer, ctDNA can be a vanishingly small share of total cfDNA - sometimes well under 1 percent, and lower still when the disease is early or nearly gone. The signal you care about is buried in an ocean of normal DNA. That is why sensitivity is everything in this field, and why deep coverage - reading each position many times over - and clever error-suppression chemistry separate the credible tests from the marketing. The whole game is detecting rare somatic fragments without being fooled by noise.

Four jobs a liquid biopsy does

"Liquid biopsy" is one technique pointed at four very different clinical problems. Keeping them straight is the key to reading the industry, because each job has its own customers, evidence bar, and economics.

Therapy selectionWhich targeted drug fits this tumor's mutations? Genotype the cancer from blood and match it to a drug, the companion diagnostic logic from Module 10.Somatic
Early detectionIs there any cancer signal in an apparently healthy person? Multi-cancer early detection (MCED) tests such as Grail's Galleri screen asymptomatic people.Somatic

Notice that three of the four read somatic DNA - the acquired mutations of a tumor - while the fourth, prenatal testing, reads germline DNA. Non-invasive prenatal testing was, in fact, the first cell-free DNA test to reach enormous scale: it uses exactly the same principle, detecting fragments of fetal DNA circulating in maternal blood, but the DNA it reads is inherited rather than cancerous. One biology, two worlds of medicine.

★ Key idea

A liquid biopsy is not a single product but a platform. The same core skill - pulling tiny amounts of the right DNA out of a tube of blood and reading it without being fooled by noise - is pointed at cancer treatment, cancer screening, cancer monitoring, and pregnancy. Master that skill and you can build several businesses from one technology.

⚠ Same blood, different bar

These four jobs are not equally hard. Genotyping a known tumor for therapy selection is comparatively easy - there is plenty of ctDNA in advanced disease. Screening a healthy person, where any cancer present is tiny and you must avoid false alarms across millions of tests, is the hardest analytical and regulatory challenge in the field. Do not assume a company strong at one job is automatically credible at another.

MRD: the most important growth category

Of the four jobs, molecular residual disease is the one investors and clinicians watch most closely, and it is worth understanding why. A therapy-selection test is usually a one-time event: you genotype the tumor, pick a drug, and move on. MRD is different. After surgery or chemotherapy, you test the patient, and then you test again, and again - monitoring over months and years to catch a recurrence the moment ctDNA reappears, often well before anything shows up on imaging. That turns a single test into a recurring, longitudinal relationship with strong clinical value, which is exactly the kind of franchise that compounds.

There are two broad ways to build an MRD test, and the difference matters. A tumor-informed approach first sequences the patient's own tumor, identifies the specific somatic variants it carries, and then builds a custom panel that hunts for precisely those variants in later blood draws. Because it knows exactly what to look for, it can be extraordinarily sensitive. Natera's Signatera is the leading example. A tumor-naive approach skips the tumor-sequencing step and instead scans the blood for a fixed, predefined set of cancer signals - faster to deploy and not dependent on a tissue sample, but generally working harder to reach the same sensitivity.

■ What actually drives the business

The science decides whether an MRD test works; payor coverage and clinical-guideline inclusion decide whether it makes money. A test that insurers reimburse and that practice guidelines tell oncologists to order becomes standard of care and scales; a brilliant test that neither covers nor recommends stays a science project. This is why diagnostics companies pour resources into reimbursement and guideline evidence, and why the recurring nature of MRD - many billable tests per patient over years - makes it the crown jewel of the category.

Where the value is going

Step back and the direction of travel is clear. The easy, early money in this industry came from therapy selection and prenatal testing - useful, established, but largely one-time tests with capped volume per patient. The frontier, and the value, is moving toward the two hardest jobs: MRD, which converts cancer care into years of recurring monitoring, and screening, which dangles the largest market of all - testing healthy people before symptoms appear. Both are where sensitivity, evidence, and reimbursement matter most, and both are where the durable franchises will be built.

✓ Key takeaways

  • cfDNA is all the DNA fragments shed into the blood; ctDNA is the small, tumor-derived, somatic fraction of it. A liquid biopsy reads this from a simple blood draw.
  • ctDNA can be far less than 1% of total cfDNA, so high sensitivity and deep sequencing coverage are what make a test credible.
  • One technique, four jobs: therapy selection, MCED screening, MRD monitoring (all somatic), and NIPT prenatal testing (germline).
  • MRD detects cancer left after treatment and catches recurrence early, often before imaging - and because it is repeated over years, it is the highest-value, most recurring category.
  • Tumor-informed MRD builds a custom panel from the patient's sequenced tumor (e.g. Signatera); tumor-naive scans a fixed panel. Payor coverage and guideline inclusion are the real commercial drivers.
Check Knowledge check

Three quick questions. Pick an answer to see the explanation.

1. What is the relationship between cfDNA and ctDNA?

cfDNA is all the cell-free DNA in the blood, most of it normal germline DNA. ctDNA is the small subset that comes from tumor cells and carries somatic variants. The tumor fraction can be well under 1% of the total, which is why sensitivity and deep coverage matter.

2. What does a molecular residual disease (MRD) test detect, and why is the category so commercially valuable?

MRD detects residual or recurring cancer from ctDNA, frequently before a scan can see it. Because the patient is monitored repeatedly over months and years, MRD turns one test into a longitudinal, recurring franchise - which is why it is the crown jewel of the field.

3. How does a tumor-informed MRD test (such as Signatera) differ from a tumor-naive one?

A tumor-informed approach sequences the patient's tumor, finds its specific somatic variants, and then hunts for exactly those in later blood draws - which makes it highly sensitive. A tumor-naive approach instead scans a fixed, predefined panel without needing the patient's tumor first.
